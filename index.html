01 — About the Research

History
that must
never
repeat

The genocide of Jews during the Second World War is becoming an ever more prominent subject of scholarly inquiry. Historians, ethnographers, philologists, theologians, philosophers, and demographers all converge on this phenomenon — seeking to understand how industrialized mass murder became state policy.

Within the "Without Statute of Limitations" project, archival institutions of Russia have prepared 23 documentary volumes that comprehensively reflect these events for the first time at scale. The Investigative Committee of the Russian Federation has opened or resumed criminal cases on mass executions of civilians across several regions.

Today's youth must understand where hatred on national grounds can lead. The dissemination of knowledge about a tragedy already committed may prevent its repetition.

02 — Research Structure

Chapter I

General Problems in the Development of the Research Topic

An examination of Holocaust historiography in Soviet and post-Soviet scholarship, tracing how ideological constraints shaped — and often suppressed — academic discourse on the Jewish genocide.

Chapter I · §1.1

Holocaust Issues in Historical Research

From Einstein's American Jewish Scholars Committee to the post-Soviet opening of archives — how the study of the Holocaust evolved across decades of political censorship and eventual scholarly reckoning.

Chapter I · §1.2

Racist Ideas of Nazi Ideology

Tracing the intellectual lineage from Gobineau's racial hierarchy to Hitler's "Mein Kampf" — how pseudoscientific race theory became the ideological engine of industrialized genocide.

Chapter II

Mass Extermination of the Jewish Population of the USSR

A region-by-region account of the Holocaust as it unfolded on Soviet territory between 1941 and 1944 — in the Baltic states, Belarus, central Russia, and the southwestern territories.

04 — Ideological Foundations

The vocabulary
of
genocide

Fascism

An ideology affirming the supremacy and exclusivity of a particular nation or race, denying democracy, establishing a cult of the leader, and justifying war as a means of resolving interstate problems.

Nazism

Where Italian fascism sought national greatness through the state, Nazism elevated the racial hierarchy to an absolute. This distinction made genocide — systematic, industrially organized annihilation of entire peoples by virtue of their origin — its primary instrument.

Holocaust

Not merely genocide in the general sense, but a systematic instrument of Nazi policy. The persecution of Jewish populations across occupied European countries. A term that has since expanded to characterize any manifestation of genocidal violence.

Racial Theory

Developed by Hitler in 1924, it declared race the primary cause of all historical events, state formations, and cultural achievements. The myth of "Aryan" supremacy justified Germany's claim to world dominance and the elimination of other peoples. Jews were positioned at the very bottom — labeled "subhumans," an "anti-race."

Conclusion

Despite significant progress in studying the Holocaust in the USSR, many aspects remain contested and require further objective research. The spread of nationalism and antisemitism in the contemporary world gives this topic particular urgency — knowledge of a tragedy already committed may prevent its recurrence.
